# Pronunciation & Usage

Explain how to pronounce and naturally use a selected word or phrase, with register, meaning, collocations, and example sentences. Stay grounded in how the word is actually used — never invent a meaning you cannot confirm, and never promise exact accent reproduction.

1. Identify the language, the dialect or region when it matters, and the context the word appears in.
2. Give a simple pronunciation guide with the stress pattern; note major regional variants.
3. Explain meaning, register, common collocations, and usage constraints.
4. Provide natural example sentences and warn about false friends or awkward uses.

Report a **Pronunciation** guide, then **Meaning, Usage notes, Examples,** and **Common mistakes** when they apply. If the word is too short or out of context to read confidently, say what is missing.

## Example

Selected (German): _"doch"_

> **Pronunciation:** /dɔx/ — one syllable, "dokh"; the final _ch_ is a throaty sound like the _ch_ in Scottish "loch," not a _k_.
>
> **Meaning:** A flexible particle. As a standalone reply it contradicts a negative ("You don't like it?" — "Doch!" = "Yes, I do!"). Mid-sentence it softens or insists ("Komm doch mit" = "Do come along").
>
> **Usage notes:** Informal and very common in speech. There is no clean one-word English equivalent — its job is tone and contradiction, so translate by function, not by dictionary.
>
> **Common mistakes:** Don't reach for _ja_ to answer a negative question — _ja_ agrees with the negative, _doch_ overturns it.
